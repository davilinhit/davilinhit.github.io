Rocket Manufacturing Capstone

June 2014 - Portland, Oregon

The Portland State Aerospace Society is a fifteen-year old rocket club that puts really interesting, sophisticated electronics to stream back live rocket flight data and video in a thick, heavy, over-engineered metal airframe. It’s occasionally been nice to have an overly durable airframe but most of the time it’s just a pain.

We got lucky enough this year to convince veterans of Portland State’s Formula SAE team to not only design a carbon fiber rocket for their senior year mechanical engineering capstone, but to develop a manufacturing process for us electrical engineers and document the hell out of it.

As far as we’re concerned, it turned into basically the best capstone ever. Their deliverables included a layup table, a 4′×4′×8′ curing oven, smaller mandrels for testing, lots of crushed and nearly-crushed modules from their strength tests, full-sized final mandrels for the final six-inch rocket, several regular rocket sections, one motor section, the retaining rings, an electronics carrier insert, prototype fins, a block of super-dense foam for the nose cone, and thousands of dollars in layup materials.

I worked with the team to develop the oven control box and I’m working with them into the summer to develop the rocket manufacturing page to store all the documentation. We’re releasing everything as open source hardware.

They brought all their deliverables out to the Engineering Building plaza for the final capstone shindig right before graduation so I went down to check it out.

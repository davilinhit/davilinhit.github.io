Replacing the IMEX Firefox guts

October 2013 - Portland, Oregon

The Open Tech Lab just bought an IMEX Firefox brushed 1/10th RC truck. I want to make an autonomous RC car that can be a vision testing platform for our quadcopter.

I’m calling this project the Foxcar.

I opened up the truck to see if I could solder my Raspberry Pi directly to the motor controller board but there were a couple chips I couldn’t identify, one of which looked like the microcontroller, so I decided to rip the guts out and replace them with my own electronics.

The Firefox has a single DC RC 550 motor and a 5-wire servo, running on a single 7.6V NiMH 1800 mAh battery. Probably this means the motor is a 6V motor. The parts were:

1x AT1206 74C02N 8-pin DIP Probably a Quad 2-input NAND, NOR, or Hex Inverter
6x 531 T70 3-pin 72 A 25V N-channel Power MOSFETs.
1x MC34063 8-pin DIP 1.5A Inverted Switching Regulator which is for the DC-DC converters.
1x D1116 16-pin DIP unknown
1x LM339L 14-pin DIP Quad-Differential Comparator
1x AMP26002 25-pin square unknown
1x MX117 8-pin DIP Electric Toy DC Motor Drive Circuit H-Bridge Drive

I took a good look at the configuration of the car. I didn’t want to replace the motor unless I had to, but the servo had to go.

This is the board. I was surprised that the antenna is just a long piece of wire tucked up inside the antenna cover. The car ran great when I tested it back before I tore it apart. Hopefully it’ll work great after it’s all put back together…

I already had an Adafruit Motor Shield and an Arduino. I hooked up a 6V DC motor and a 3-wire servo just to be sure it worked. I swapped servos on the car and hooked up the Arduino.

My other 6V DC motor ran happily on the Adafruit example sketch but this motor just clicks in one direction or the other. I tried swapping the red and black wires (though it’s bi-directional, so not sure why that would make a difference) and I checked to be sure it has the three capacitors soldered to the motor itself for noise control.

So figuring out the DC motor is the next piece. My three-wire servo works great, though.

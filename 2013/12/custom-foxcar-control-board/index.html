Foxcar Control Board v1

December 2013 - Portland, Oregon

It’s time to add a control board to my RC car. My goal is a car that does basically everything by itself so I looked into robot exploration algorithms and figured out I need to have localization (where am I?), mapping (what’s around me?), and planning (where am I going?). I broke up the design of the control board into four parts: power and motors, microcontroller, networking and logging, and all the sensor inputs.

I scrounged up sensors in the Open Tech Lab, where I originally scrounged up the car, and came up with a couple of rangefinders, a PX4FLOW, a 9DOF sensor stick with accel/gyro/mag, and a CMOS camera which has no markings except for its chip, a VC0706.

I knew I’d want an output for the servo now but I also wanted to provide the same interface for ESC PWM signals as I had on my previous quadcopter control board. Ideally this board could go on a quad without any modifications. As for power, I already knew that the VNH5019 carrier board from Pololu works fine for my car so I designed the board for over 20A of stall current.

I added a micro-USB connector and hooked the 5V through a diode to the linear regulator so I can’t power the car from USB (it’s not big enough to carry a laptop anyway) but I’ll be able to debug directly.

I decided I’d also want logging via a microSD card and networked control over XBees I also have on hand. Overall, the system looks like this.

I chose the STM32F4 because PSAS is using it so one of my other boards is using it, I’ve already got a dev environment for it, I’d like to use ChibiOS, and it’s basically capable of anything I would want for its size. It’s also ubiquitous so there are tons of help forums and it’s got all the pins, DAC, and memory I could want.

I got this far and was looking into which IMU sensors to buy when I talked to a guy who does IMUs who flipped out when I suggested writing a Kalman filter. He stressed that I could do whatever the hell I wanted but he asked if what I really wanted was really to actually spend a year writing a decent IMU, really? really? think hard now… and I agreed, okay, no, I want to make my car drive around and get into trouble.

I also realized a basic requirement of this car is to use optical flow instead of GPS for absolute position correction. This is the root problem I’m interested in and I’ve started to read up on possible solutions.

I went looking at the other autopilot boards to find out which sensors they’re using, then I looked up open source IMUs which already have ports to the STM32F4, and I decided to use FreeIMU with the MPU6000 and HMC5883L. Note that originally I figured I’d use the MPU6050 and then realized that the 6000 comes with SPI as well as I2C so the 6000 is the better deal for the faster speed.

I went back to my pile of sensors, took the STM32F4, broke out all the peripherals I could use, and matched up sensors to the chip. I hooked up the compass and Invensense gyro/accelometer chip, then broke all the other GPIO points left over into as many header as I could. Finally, I worked up a schematic using my proven previous design for the XBee, USB, and SD card.

I started the schematic on November 22nd and finished on December 15th, which was exactly on target. I worked in two crunch weeks, the first for the schematic and the second for layout, with a break between them to let the schematic linger a little.

The final schematics and boards are up on Github.

The final 4-layer board comes out to 3.5×2.2 inches for an OSHPark cost of $77 for three.
